About Exoplanets

The exploration of exoplanets has evolved significantly over the years, marked by key milestones such as the first confirmed exoplanets discovered in 1992 around the pulsar PSR B1257+12 and the groundbreaking identification of 51 Pegasi b, a "hot Jupiter," in 1995. The launch of the Kepler Space Telescope in 2009 revolutionized the field by uncovering thousands of exoplanets, including the notable TRAPPIST-1 system in 2017, which features seven Earth-sized planets. The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), launched in 2018, continues this exploration by focusing on brighter stars. Meanwhile, the James Webb Space Telescope, launched in 2021, aims to analyze exoplanet atmospheres for potential biosignatures. Key detection methods include the transit method, radial velocity, direct imaging, and gravitational microlensing. As technology advances, research is increasingly focused on understanding planetary formation, composition, and the potential for life beyond Earth.

What are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. They vary widely in size, composition, and distance from their host stars. Some are gas giants similar to Jupiter, while others are rocky like Earth. Exoplanets can be found in a range of environments, from those orbiting within their star's habitable zone, where conditions might allow for liquid water, to those in extreme climates far from their stars.

Exoplanet Discovery Timeline

1995

In 1995, the first exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star, 51 Pegasi b, was discovered by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz. This marked a groundbreaking moment in astronomy, as it was the first confirmed detection of a planet outside our solar system orbiting a star similar to our Sun. This discovery launched the modern era of exoplanet research and opened the door to finding many more planets beyond our solar system..

2009

In 2009, NASA launched the Kepler Space Telescope, which revolutionized the search for exoplanets. Using the transit method, Kepler monitored over 150,000 stars, looking for tiny dips in brightness caused by planets passing in front of them. This mission led to the discovery of thousands of exoplanets, including Earth-sized planets in habitable zones, greatly expanding our understanding of planetary systems beyond our own. Kepler's findings continue to influence exoplanet research today..

2018

In 2018, NASA launched the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), following the success of the Kepler mission. TESS is designed to search for exoplanets around the brightest stars near Earth using the transit method. Unlike Kepler, which focused on a small patch of the sky, TESS surveys nearly the entire sky, with the goal of finding Earth-like planets in nearby star systems. TESS has already discovered hundreds of exoplanets and continues to be a major tool in identifying potential planets for future study, particularly those that could support life.

Exoplanet Missions

Kepler Mission

Launched in 2009, the Kepler space telescope was NASA's first mission capable of finding Earth-size planets around other stars. The spacecraft detected exoplanets using the transit method, measuring the tiny dip in a star's brightness that occurs when a planet crosses in front of it.

TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite)

Launched in 2018, TESS is designed to search for exoplanets using the transit method in an area 400 times larger than that covered by the Kepler mission. It focuses on the brightest stars near Earth, making follow-up studies easier.

Learn More

James Webb Space Telescope

Launched in 2021, the James Webb Space Telescope is not primarily an exoplanet mission, but its powerful infrared capabilities make it an excellent tool for studying exoplanet atmospheres and compositions.
